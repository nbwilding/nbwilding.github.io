Revision of probability and probability distributions - Problem set 1

1.1 Properties of the binomial distribution

Write down the form of the binomial distribution \(P_n\) giving the probability of observing precisely \(n\) instances of an event in a total of \(N\) trials each of which may give that event with probability \(p\).

Write down expressions for the mean \(\bar{n} \equiv \sum_{n=0}^{N} n P_n\) and variance \(\Delta n^2 \equiv \sum_{n=0}^{N} (n - \bar{n})^2 P_n\) of this distribution.

1.2 Thinking about probabilities

  1. What is the probability that in \(N\) trials, each with probability of an event \(p\), one sees at least one instance of an event?

  2. When is \(1 - \exp(-Np)\) a good approximation to this probability?

  3. When is \(Np\) a good approximation to this probability?

1.3 The Gaussian distribution

Consider the binomial distribution discussed above. Use Stirling’s approximation to show that

\[ s(n) \equiv \ln P_n \simeq n \ln p + (N - n) \ln (1 - p) + N \ln N - (N - n) \ln (N - n) - n \ln n \]

for large \(N, n\).

Show that

\[ s'(n) = \ln p - \ln (1 - p) + \ln (N - n) - \ln n \]

\[ s''(n) = - \frac{N}{n (N - n)} \]

Now make a Taylor expansion to second order in \(x = n - Np\) to give

\[ s(x) \simeq s(Np) - \frac{x^2}{2N(1 - p)p} \]

(why is there no linear term in \(x\)?).

Thus deduce

\[ P_n \simeq P_{Np} \exp\left( - \frac{(n - Np)^2}{2N(1 - p)p} \right) \]

1.4 The random walk

An inebriated customer exits a pub and lurches from one lamp post to another on his way home; at each lamp post he pauses for reflection before starting out again. Each time he starts he is equally likely to move towards or away from home. If the posts are separated by a distance \(a\), find the mean and the standard deviation of his displacement \(d\) from the starting point, after \(N\) steps (i.e. encounters with a lamppost).

In fact the random walk plays a key role in our understanding of how randomness affects our lives. See “The Drunkards walk” by L. Mlodinow

1.5 Contrasting one and many

Consider a system of \(N\) particles, each of which can exist in either of two states, of energies \(0\) and \(\hat{\epsilon}\). Suppose that each particle is equally likely to be in either of its two states. Write down expressions for:

  • the mean energy of one particle, and its standard deviation;
  • the mean energy of the system, and its standard deviation.

1.6 Practically never

Consider a cubic box of side \(L = 1\)m containing \(N = 6 \times 10^{23}\) molecules. Estimate the probability of finding all the molecules in the left-hand half of the box.

How long might you expect to wait before observing one of the special arrangements whose probability you have just estimated.
