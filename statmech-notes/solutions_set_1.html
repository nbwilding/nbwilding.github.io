1.1 Properties of the binomial distribution

The binomial distribution of probabilities is given by

\[ p_n = {{N}\choose{n}} p^n q^{(N-n)} \quad \text{where} \quad {{N}\choose{n}} \equiv \frac{N!}{(N-n)! n!} \]

is the binomial coefficient giving the number of distinct ways of sorting \(N\) objects into two piles containing \(n\) and \(N-n\) respectively.

The distribution approaches a Gaussian for large enough \(N\) (even \(N \simeq 10^2\) is large enough for this correspondence to be good).

The most important things to know about this distribution (apart from what it means) are its mean and variance, which are given by

\[ \bar{n} \equiv \sum_{n=0}^N n p_n = Np \quad \text{(mean)} \]

and

\[ \Delta n^2 \equiv \sum_{n=0}^N (n - \bar{n})^2 p_n = Npq \quad \text{(variance)} \]

The standard deviation (which measures the width of the distribution) is identified as the square root of the variance. It is important to know these results!

1.2 Thinking about probabilities

1. The probability of not seeing the event in a single trial is \(1 - p\). Therefore, the probability of not seeing the event in all \(N\) trials (i.e., zero occurrences of the event) is:

\[ P(\text{no event}) = (1 - p)^N \]

Thus, the probability of seeing at least one instance of the event is the complement of this:

\[ P(\text{at least one event}) = 1 - (1 - p)^N \]

2. For small values of \(p\) and large \(N\), the expression \((1 - p)^N\) can be approximated using the exponential function. Specifically, for small \(p\), we use the approximation \((1 - p) \approx e^{-p}\). Applying this to \((1 - p)^N\), we get:

\[ (1 - p)^N \approx \exp(-Np) \]

Thus, the probability of seeing at least one instance becomes:

\[ P(\text{at least one event}) \approx 1 - \exp(-Np) \]

This approximation works well when \(Np\) is small, meaning when either \(p\) is small or \(N\) is large but \(p\) remains small enough that the product \(Np\) is not large.

3. \(Np\) approximates the expected number of events (mean of the binomial distribution). For small probabilities, \(Np\) can be used to estimate the likelihood of observing at least one event. This is particularly effective when \(Np\) is small because:

  1. When \(p\) is very small and \(N\) is reasonably large, the distribution becomes approximately Poisson with mean \(Np\), and the probability of at least one event is close to \(Np\).
  2. When \(Np\) is small, the expression \(1 - \exp(-Np) \approx Np\), as \(\exp(-Np)\) can be expanded for small \(Np\).

Hence, \(Np\) is a good approximation for the probability of at least one event when \(p\) is small and \(Np\) is small.

1.3 The Gaussian distribution

Start by writing the logarithm of the binomial probability \(P_n\):

\[ s(n) = \ln P_n = \ln N! - \ln (N-n)! - \ln n! + n \ln p + (N - n) \ln (1-p) \]

Stirling’s approximation is given by:

\[ \ln N! \simeq N \ln N - N \]

Using this for \(N!\), \((N-n)!\), and \(n!\), we get:

\[ \ln N! \simeq N \ln N - N \] \[ \ln (N - n)! \simeq (N - n) \ln (N - n) - (N - n) \] \[ \ln n! \simeq n \ln n - n \]

Thus

\[ s(n) = \left( N \ln N - N \right) - \left( (N-n) \ln (N-n) - (N-n) \right) - \left( n \ln n - n \right) + n \ln p + (N - n) \ln (1 - p) \]

Simplifying:

\[ s(n) \simeq n \ln p + (N - n) \ln (1 - p) + N \ln N - (N - n) \ln (N - n) - n \ln n \]

Next we compute the first and second derivatives of \(s(n)\) with respect to \(n\).

\[ s'(n) = \frac{\partial}{\partial n} \left[ n \ln p + (N - n) \ln (1 - p) + N \ln N - (N - n) \ln (N - n) - n \ln n \right] \]

Differentiating term by term:

\[ s'(n) = \ln p - \ln (1 - p) + \ln (N - n) - \ln n \]

Then

\[ s''(n) = \frac{\partial}{\partial n} \left( \ln p - \ln (1 - p) + \ln (N - n) - \ln n \right) \] so

\[ s''(n) = -\frac{1}{N - n} - \frac{1}{n} = -\frac{N}{n(N - n)} \]

The question invites us to perform a Taylor Expansion in \(x = n - Np\). We expand \(s(n)\) to second order around \(n = Np\):

\[ s(n) \simeq s(Np) + s'(Np)(n - Np) + \frac{1}{2} s''(Np) (n - Np)^2 \]

At \(n = Np\), the first derivative \(s'(Np) = 0\), so the linear term vanishes. Thus, the second-order expansion is:

\[ s(n) \simeq s(Np) + \frac{1}{2} s''(Np) (n - Np)^2 \]

Substitute \(s''(Np) = -\frac{1}{Np(1 - p)}\):

\[ s(n) \simeq s(Np) - \frac{(n - Np)^2}{2N(1 - p)p} \]

Finally, we can exponentiate both sides to recover \(P_n\):

\[ P_n \simeq P_{Np} \exp\left( - \frac{(n - Np)^2}{2N(1 - p)p} \right) \] showing that the binomial distribution is approximated by a Gaussian distribution.

1.4 The random walk

Denote by \(n_R\) the number of steps taken to the right (the homeward direction, say). The number taken to the left is then \(n_L = N - n_R\). The displacement \(d\) from the starting point may thus be identified as

\[ d = (n_R - n_L)a = (2n_R - N)a \]

Now a step to the right occurs on each “trial” (i.e. some step) with probability \(p = 1/2\). Thus the distribution of the number \(n_R\) of steps to the right is binomial with mean and standard deviation

\[ \overline{n_R} = Np = N/2 \quad \text{and} \quad \sqrt{\overline{\Delta n_R^2}} = \sqrt{Npq} = \sqrt{N/4} \]

The mean and standard deviation of the displacement follow as

\[ \bar{d} = (2\overline{n_R} - N)a = 0 \]

and, since \(\Delta d \equiv d - \bar{d} = 2\Delta n_R a\),

\[ \sqrt{\overline{\Delta d^2}} = 2a \sqrt{\overline{\Delta n_R^2}} = \sqrt{Na} \]

The typical distance from the origin thus increases as the square root of the number of steps taken.

Though couched in whimsical language this little calculation has a wide range of applications (it is the basic model of a diffusive process) and can be extended and refined in many interesting ways (look up “Random Walk” on the internet!). One thing you might try is extending the above argument to a biased walk in which a step to the right is more likely than a step to the left.

1.5 Contrasting one and many

The mean energy of a single particle is

\[ \bar{\varepsilon} = \sum_{i=1}^{2} p_i \varepsilon_i \]

where (in this case) \(p_1 = p_2 = \frac{1}{2}\). Thus

\[ \bar{\varepsilon} = 0 \times \frac{1}{2} + \hat{\varepsilon} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}}{2} \]

The variance of the energy of a single particle is

\[ \overline{(\Delta \varepsilon)^2} = \sum_{i=1}^{2} p_i (\varepsilon_i - \bar{\varepsilon})^2 = \left( - \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}}{2} \right)^2 \times \frac{1}{2} + \left( \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}}{2} \right)^2 \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}^2}{4} \]

So the standard deviation of the energy of one particle is

\[ \left[ \overline{(\Delta \varepsilon)^2 }\right]^{1/2} = \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}}{2} \]

The energy of \(N\) particles can be written as \(E = n \hat{\varepsilon}\) where \(n\) is the number of particles in excited states. Then, appealing to the binomial distribution (with \(p = q = \frac{1}{2}\)),

\[ \overline{E} = \overline{n} \hat{\varepsilon} = N p \hat{\varepsilon} = N \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}}{2} \]

and

\[ \overline{(\Delta E)^2} = \overline{(\Delta n)^2 }\hat{\varepsilon}^2 = N p q \hat{\varepsilon}^2 = N \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}^2}{4} \]

so that

\[ \left[ \overline{(\Delta E)^2} \right]^{1/2} = \sqrt{N} \frac{\hat{\varepsilon}}{2} \]

The most pertinent comment is that while the standard deviation of the energy of a single particle is “significant” on the scale of its mean

\[ \frac{\left[\overline{ (\Delta \varepsilon)^2} \right]^{1/2}}{\bar{\varepsilon}} = 1 \]

the standard deviation of the energy of the system as a whole is vanishingly small on the scale of its mean

\[ \frac{\left[ \overline{(\Delta E)^2 }\right]^{1/2}}{E} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \]

This is a simple instance of a fundamental principle: physical properties are ever more sharply defined the more independent contributions they comprise.

1.6 Practically never

The information supplied suggests that the distribution of the molecules over the two halves of the box will be refreshed roughly every \(\tau \sim 10^3 \, \text{s}\). Suppose we look through \(\mathcal{N}\) such arrangements, at intervals of \(\tau\). In each case we will have probability \(p_N\) of finding the ‘special’ arrangement. Thus in \(\mathcal{N}\) occasions the mean number giving the special arrangement is \(p_N \mathcal{N}\) (the mean of the binomial distribution).

To give ourselves a reasonable chance of encountering such an arrangement, we need to have \(\mathcal{N}\) such that \(p_N \mathcal{N} \sim 1\). Thus we need \(\mathcal{N} \sim 1 / p_N\), which will require a time \(\mathcal{N} \tau \sim \tau / p_N\), which is something like \(10^{(2 \times 10^{23})} \, \text{s}\) (the value assigned to \(\tau\) gets lost!!). By comparison, the age of the universe is \(\sim 10^{17} \, \text{s}\).
