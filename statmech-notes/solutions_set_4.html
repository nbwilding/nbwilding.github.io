4.1 Statistical mechanics of the 1d harmonic oscillator

The analysis is given in chapter 8. For high \(T\), in the sense that \(\hbar \omega / kT \ll 1\), we can write

\[ e^{\hbar \omega / kT} \simeq 1 + \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} + O\left( \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} \right)^2 \]

so that

\[ \bar{n} = \frac{1}{1 + \hbar \omega / kT + \dots - 1} = \frac{kT}{\hbar \omega} \gg 1 \]

whence \(\bar{\varepsilon} \simeq kT\). The disappearance of \(\hbar\) manifests the fact that this result is “classical”: it can be established by arguments which make no reference to the underlying quantum theory of the harmonic oscillator; they capture the high \(T\) behaviour correctly, but fail to predict the different behaviour that sets in, typically, as \(kT\) becomes small enough to “resolve” the discreteness of the energy level structure.

This result is an example of the Equipartition Theorem.

4.2 Entropy of the vibrating solid

The partition function for the 1d oscillator is (Chapter 8.2)

\[ Z^{d=1}(1) = \frac{e^{-x/2}}{1 - e^{-x}} \quad \text{with} \quad x = \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} \]

The partition function for \(3N\) 1d oscillators (or \(N\) 3d oscillators) is

\[ Z = \left[ Z^{d=1}(1) \right]^{3N} \]

The mean energy of one 1d oscillator is

\[ \bar{\varepsilon}^{d=1} = \left[ \frac{1}{e^x - 1} + \frac{1}{2} \right] \hbar \omega \]

and the mean energy of \(3N\) such oscillators is

\[ \bar{E} = 3N \left[ \frac{1}{e^x - 1} + \frac{1}{2} \right] \hbar \omega \]

Appealing to the general result (Key Point 11)

\[ S = k \ln Z + \frac{\bar{E}}{T} \]

We then have

\[ S = 3Nk \left( \ln \left[ \frac{e^{-x/2}}{1 - e^{-x}} \right] + \left[ \frac{1}{e^x - 1} + \frac{1}{2} \right] \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} \right) \]

Simplifying,

\[ S = 3Nk \left( \frac{x}{e^x - 1} - \ln \left[ 1 - e^{-x} \right] \right) \]

For low \(T\) (\(x \gg 1\)) we have

\[ \frac{x}{e^x - 1} \simeq xe^{-x} \quad \text{and} \quad \ln \left[ 1 - e^{-x} \right] \simeq -e^{-x} \]

The first of these terms dominates the second, so that

\[ S \simeq 3Nkxe^{-x} \]

which vanishes exponentially (as \(e^{-\hbar \omega / kT}\)) as \(T \to 0\).

For high \(T\) (\(x \ll 1\)),

\[ \frac{x}{e^x - 1} \simeq 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \ln \left[ 1 - e^{-x} \right] \simeq \ln x \]

This time the second term is the dominant one, so that

\[ S \simeq -3Nk \ln x = 3Nk \ln \left( \frac{kT}{\hbar \omega} \right) \]

In contrast to the magnet, the vibrational entropy continues to grow with increasing \(T\) (as \(\ln T\)), manifesting the fact that the oscillators explore ever higher parts of their energy-level ladder as the temperature grows. Correspondingly, the heat capacity

\[ C_V \equiv T \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial T} \right)_V = T \frac{\partial}{\partial T} \left( 3Nk \ln T \right) = 3Nk \]

This is the Dulong and Petit law; the limiting non-zero value for \(C_V\) is to be compared with the result for the magnet, where the heat capacity vanishes as \(T \to \infty\).

4.3 A check on the entropy formula

According to the result established chapter 10.3, the entropy of one mole (\(N = N_A\)) of ideal gas in volume \(V\) at temperature \(T\) is

\[ S(T) = R \left[ \ln \left( \frac{2\pi MkT}{h^2} \right)^{\frac{3}{2}} - \ln \left( \frac{N}{V} \right) + \frac{5}{2} \right] \]

or

\[ S = R \left[ \frac{3}{2} \ln T - \ln \left( \frac{N}{V} \right) + \frac{5}{2} + \frac{3}{2} \ln \left( \frac{2\pi Mk}{h^2} \right) \right] \]

Now, setting \(M = 6.62 \times 10^{-26} \, \text{kg}\), \(kT_b = 1.205 \times 10^{-21} \, \text{J}\), and \(V/N \equiv V/N_A = kT/P = 1.18 \times 10^{-26} \, \text{m}^3\) (with \(P = 1.013 \times 10^5 \, \text{Nm}^{-2}\)), we find \(S(T_b) \simeq 129.2 \, \text{J mol}^{-1} \, \text{K}^{-1}\), in excellent agreement with the measured value.
