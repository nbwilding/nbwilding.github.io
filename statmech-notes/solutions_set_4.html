4.1 Statistical mechanics of the 1d harmonic oscillator

The analysis is given in chapter 8. For high \(T\), in the sense that \(\hbar \omega / kT \ll 1\), we can write

\[ e^{\hbar \omega / kT} \simeq 1 + \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} + O\left( \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} \right)^2 \]

so that

\[ \bar{n} = \frac{1}{1 + \hbar \omega / kT + \dots - 1} = \frac{kT}{\hbar \omega} \gg 1 \]

whence \(\overline{\varepsilon} \simeq kT\). The disappearance of \(\hbar\) manifests the fact that this result is “classical”: it can be established by arguments which make no reference to the underlying quantum theory of the harmonic oscillator; they capture the high \(T\) behaviour correctly, but fail to predict the different behaviour that sets in, typically, as \(kT\) becomes small enough to “resolve” the discreteness of the energy level structure.

This result is an example of the Equipartition Theorem.

4.2 Entropy of the vibrating solid

The partition function for the 1d oscillator is (Chapter 8.2)

\[ Z^{d=1}(1) = \frac{e^{-x/2}}{1 - e^{-x}} \quad \text{with} \quad x = \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} \]

The partition function for \(3N\) 1d oscillators (or \(N\) 3d oscillators) is

\[ Z = \left[ Z^{d=1}(1) \right]^{3N} \]

The mean energy of one 1d oscillator is

\[ \overline{\varepsilon}^{d=1} = \left[ \frac{1}{e^x - 1} + \frac{1}{2} \right] \hbar \omega \]

and the mean energy of \(3N\) such oscillators is

\[ \overline{E} = 3N \left[ \frac{1}{e^x - 1} + \frac{1}{2} \right] \hbar \omega \]

Appealing to the general result (Key Point 11)

\[ S = k \ln Z + \frac{\bar{E}}{T} \]

We then have

\[ S = 3Nk \left( \ln \left[ \frac{e^{-x/2}}{1 - e^{-x}} \right] + \left[ \frac{1}{e^x - 1} + \frac{1}{2} \right] \frac{\hbar \omega}{kT} \right) \]

Simplifying,

\[ S = 3Nk \left( \frac{x}{e^x - 1} - \ln \left[ 1 - e^{-x} \right] \right) \]

For low \(T\) (\(x \gg 1\)) we have

\[ \frac{x}{e^x - 1} \simeq xe^{-x} \quad \text{and} \quad \ln \left[ 1 - e^{-x} \right] \simeq -e^{-x} \]

The first of these terms dominates the second, so that

\[ S \simeq 3Nkxe^{-x} \]

which vanishes exponentially (as \(e^{-\hbar \omega / kT}\)) as \(T \to 0\).

For high \(T\) (\(x \ll 1\)),

\[ \frac{x}{e^x - 1} \simeq 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \ln \left[ 1 - e^{-x} \right] \simeq \ln x \]

This time the second term is the dominant one, so that

\[ S \simeq -3Nk \ln x = 3Nk \ln \left( \frac{kT}{\hbar \omega} \right) \]

In contrast to the magnet, the vibrational entropy continues to grow with increasing \(T\) (as \(\ln T\)), manifesting the fact that the oscillators explore ever higher parts of their energy-level ladder as the temperature grows. Correspondingly, the heat capacity

\[ C_V \equiv T \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial T} \right)_V = T \frac{\partial}{\partial T} \left( 3Nk \ln T \right) = 3Nk \]

This is the Dulong and Petit law; the limiting non-zero value for \(C_V\) is to be compared with the result for the magnet, where the heat capacity vanishes as \(T \to \infty\).

4.3 A check on the entropy formula

According to the result established chapter 10.3, the entropy of one mole (\(N = N_A\)) of ideal gas in volume \(V\) at temperature \(T\) is

\[ S(T) = R \left[ \ln \left( \frac{2\pi MkT}{h^2} \right)^{\frac{3}{2}} - \ln \left( \frac{N}{V} \right) + \frac{5}{2} \right] \]

or

\[ S = R \left[ \frac{3}{2} \ln T - \ln \left( \frac{N}{V} \right) + \frac{5}{2} + \frac{3}{2} \ln \left( \frac{2\pi Mk}{h^2} \right) \right] \]

Now, setting \(M = 6.62 \times 10^{-26} \, \text{kg}\), \(kT_b = 1.205 \times 10^{-21} \, \text{J}\), and \(V/N \equiv V/N_A = kT/P = 1.18 \times 10^{-26} \, \text{m}^3\) (with \(P = 1.013 \times 10^5 \, \text{Nm}^{-2}\)), we find \(S(T_b) \simeq 129.2 \, \text{J mol}^{-1} \, \text{K}^{-1}\), in excellent agreement with the measured value.

4.4 Density of states for a 2D gas

The solutions to the Schrödinger equation for a particle confined to 2 dimensions are of the form:

\[ \phi(x, y) = B \sin(k_x x) \sin(k_y y) \quad \text{with} \quad k_x = \frac{n_x \pi}{L} \quad k_y = \frac{n_y \pi}{L} \]

where \(n_x, n_y = 1, 2, 3 \dots\). We define

\[ \Gamma(k) dk \equiv \text{number of solutions with k-values in the range} \ k \to k + dk \]

This quantity is identifiable (refer to the figure in Appendix C) as the number of allowed points in \(k\)-space lying in the shell bounded by circles of radii \(k\) and \(k + dk\). This is

\[ \Gamma(k) dk = \frac{2\pi k dk}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{(\pi / L)^2} \]

The first factor is the area of one quadrant of the shell; the second factor is the reciprocal of the area of the space associated with each point. Reorganizing, and identifying \(A = L^2\), gives

\[ \Gamma(k) dk = \frac{A k dk}{2\pi} \]

Recalling the definition of the density of states,

\[ g(\epsilon) d\epsilon \equiv \text{number of states with energies in the range} \ \epsilon \to \epsilon + d\epsilon \]

we identify

\[ g(\epsilon) d\epsilon = \Gamma(k) dk \quad \text{with} \quad \epsilon = \frac{\hbar^2 k^2}{2M} \quad \text{and} \quad d\epsilon = \frac{\hbar^2 k dk}{M} \]

Then

\[ g(\epsilon) d\epsilon = \frac{A}{2\pi} \cdot k dk = \frac{A}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{M}{\hbar^2} d\epsilon \]

so that

\[ g(\epsilon) = \frac{AM}{2\pi \hbar^2} \]

which is to be contrasted with the result for 3d where \(g(\epsilon)\) increases as \(\epsilon^{1/2}\).

The 2d gas is not just a theorist’s fantasy: there are a range of physical systems (atoms absorbed onto substrates, electrons in “quantum wells”, \(\dots\)) to which this theory is relevant.

4.5 The Fermi-Dirac distribution

The Fermi-Dirac distribution is defined by

\[ f_+(\epsilon) = \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\epsilon - \mu)} + 1} \]

It gives the mean number, \(\bar{n}\), of fermions in a given state of energy \(\epsilon\). The distribution encodes the exclusion principle which asserts that one “state” (one solution to the single-particle Schrödinger equation) can accommodate at most one fermion.

Let \(p_o(\epsilon)\) denote the probability that a particular state of energy \(\epsilon\) is occupied. The probability it is unoccupied (“empty”) is then \(p_e(\epsilon) = 1 - p_o(\epsilon)\). The mean number of fermions in this state can then be written as

\[ \bar{n} = 0 \times p_e(\epsilon) + 1 \times p_o(\epsilon) = p_o(\epsilon) \]

whence \(f_+(\epsilon) = p_o(\epsilon)\) as claimed. Note that, consistent with this identification, the function \(f_+(\epsilon)\) lies between 0 and 1.

The relevant plots at \(T=0\) and low \(T>0\) are shownin chapter 13.

4.6 Energy of the electron gas

The mean total energy of a free electron (fermi) gas can be written as a sum over states:

\[ \overline{E} = \sum_i \overline{n_i} \epsilon_i \]

where \(\overline{n_i} \equiv f_+(\epsilon_i)\) is the mean number of electrons in state \(i\) of energy \(\epsilon_i\). The sum can be replaced by an integral, using the density of states:

\[ \overline{E} = \int_0^{\infty} d\epsilon \, g(\epsilon) f_+(\epsilon) \epsilon \]

which is the expression quoted. At \(T = 0\), \(f_+(\epsilon)\) assumes the simple form

\[ f_+(\epsilon) = \begin{cases} 1 & \epsilon < \epsilon_f \\ 0 & \epsilon > \epsilon_f \end{cases} \]

and the energy assumes the form

\[ \overline{E}(T = 0) = \int_0^{\epsilon_f} d\epsilon \, g(\epsilon) \epsilon \]

It is useful to set this equation alongside the equation which determines \(\epsilon_f \equiv \mu (T = 0)\), namely

\[ N = \int_0^{\epsilon_f} d\epsilon \, g(\epsilon) \]

Now we need only recall the form of \(g(\epsilon)\) from chapter 10, namely

\[ g(\epsilon) = \tilde{D} V \epsilon^{1/2} \]

Then, dividing the equation for \(\bar{E}\) by the equation for \(N\) we obtain,

\[ \frac{\overline{E}(T = 0)}{N} = \frac{\int_0^{\epsilon_f} d\epsilon \, \epsilon^{3/2}}{\int_0^{\epsilon_f} d\epsilon \, \epsilon^{1/2}} = \frac{3}{5} \epsilon_f \]

from which the constant \(\tilde{D}\) has conveniently disappeared. Thus

\[ \overline{E}(T = 0) = \frac{3N}{5} \epsilon_f \]

4.7 Ideal Fermi gas model of conduction electrons in Na

The number density (number-per-unit-volume) of electrons in the Na conduction electron gas is

\[ n_e \equiv \frac{N}{V} = \frac{6.02 \times 10^{23}}{23.7 \times 10^{-6}} = 2.54 \times 10^{28} \, m^{-3} \]

The Fermi energy is determined by (cf. Key Point 19)

\[ \epsilon_f = \frac{\hbar^2 k_f^2}{2M} \]

where

\[ k_f \equiv (3 n_e \pi^2)^{1/3} \]

is the magnitude of the wavevector of electrons with the Fermi energy. Inserting the value of \(n_e\), and the free-electron mass, we find \(\epsilon_f = 3.15 \, \text{eV}\), which is typical of simple metals. It is large compared to \(kT\) at any reasonable temperature.
