    14. The ideal bose gas

    In this chapter, we will be dealing with bosons for which the relevant distribution is the Bose-Einstein distribution:

    \[ n(\epsilon) = f_B(\epsilon) = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{\epsilon - \mu}{kT}\right) - 1} \]

    Recall that this gives the average number of bosons in a quantum state of energy \(\epsilon\).

    For bosons, there is no exclusion principle (so no restriction on the number of bosons in a quantum state); however, there are still strong effects of indistinguishability

    14.1 Quanta as Bosons

    A quantum harmonic oscillator of frequency \(\omega\) has energy levels:

    \[ \epsilon_n = \hbar \omega \left( n + \frac{1}{2} \right) \]

    Recall from chapter 9 that for such an oscillator in contact with a heat reservoir:

    \[ n = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{\hbar \omega}{kT}\right) - 1} \]

    Comparing this with the Bose-Einstein distribution above, we see that the average energy level \(n\) of the quantum oscillator can be thought of as the average number of bosons in a quantum state with energy \(\hbar \omega\) and chemical potential \(\mu = 0\).

    Thus, quanta of energy can be treated as bosons. This is reasonable since there is no restriction on the number of quanta of energy in the oscillator, and the quanta of energy clearly cannot be labeled and are indistinguishable.

    That \(\mu = 0\) expresses the fact that there is no constraint on the number of quanta, i.e., the number of quanta is not conserved. Returning to a definition of \(\mu\) from chapter 14:

    \[ \mu = \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial N}\right)_{T, V} \]

    we see that \(\mu = 0\) implies that we minimize the free energy with respect to \(N\) (number of quanta).

    Now, proceed to use the idea of quanta as bosons to view the wave/particle duality you will have encountered in quantum mechanics courses in a new light. For our purposes, we consider only standing waves.

    Wave/Particle Duality

    1. The standing waves or normal modes of the system have discrete frequencies \(\nu\) (or angular frequencies \(\omega\)). We think of them as the quantum states.
    2. Quantum mechanics tells us energy is quantized, thus the energy \(\epsilon\) in a mode is \(n h \nu + \text{constant}\).
    3. These quanta can be treated as bosons; therefore, the average number of quanta in a quantum state is given by the B-E distribution \(n(\nu) = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{h \nu}{kT}\right) - 1}\). This will tell us how energy is distributed amongst the possible frequencies.

    If you are unsure of how it all fits together, a simple example might help: consider the 1D wave equation

    \[ \frac{1}{v^2} \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial t^2} = \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} \]

    This is solved (for boundary conditions that vanish at \(0\), \(L\)) by the substitution \(\psi = T(t) X(x)\) with \(X = \sin(n \pi x / L)\):

    \[ \frac{1}{v^2} \frac{d^2 T}{dt^2} = - k^2 T \]

    where \(k = n \pi / L\), which is just the equation for a harmonic oscillator with angular frequency \(\omega = k v\).

    Therefore, quantum mechanically, the wave modes should be treated as quantum oscillators with angular frequency \(\omega\) or frequency \(\nu\), which in turn can be treated as Bose states with the energy quanta as bosons.

    14.2 Blackbody or Cavity Radiation

    A “blackbody” is one that absorbs all incident radiation (none is reflected). Thus, when cold, a blackbody looks black. However, if the body is not to heat up indefinitely, it eventually must radiate as much energy as it absorbs. Thus, a hot blackbody does not look black because it is emitting radiation. The question is as to the spectrum of the emitted radiation.

    Historically, this was an important question since empirically it seemed that the radiation spectrum depended only on temperature, not the composition of the black body. Also, a classical description ran into a problem known as the “ultraviolet catastrophe” to be discussed below.

    The idealized model of blackbody radiation is to think of the radiation inside a cavity (like an oven) at temperature \(T\) as being in equilibrium. If a very small hole is drilled in the cavity, the radiation that escapes will be the same as blackbody radiation and will have the same spectrum as the radiation in equilibrium inside the cavity.

    The radiation inside the cavity is just made up of electromagnetic standing waves (or normal modes) of frequency \(\nu\). Treating the photons as bosons as above and using the B-E distribution, we have for the average energy in a mode of frequency \(\nu\):

    \[ \epsilon(\nu) = h \nu n(\nu) = \frac{h \nu}{\exp\left(\frac{h \nu}{kT}\right) - 1} \]

    We just need to calculate the density of modes in the same way we calculated the density of states for a free particle in a box. In fact, the calculation is very nearly identical. We reproduce it for completeness.

    Consider a cubic cavity side \(L\); to have the wave vanish at the boundaries, we must have \(L \nu_x = 2 n_x c\).

    The momentum component of the wave in the \(x\)-direction is

    \[ p_x = \frac{h \nu_x}{c} = \frac{h n_x}{2L} \]

    and the magnitude of the momentum is

    \[ p = \frac{h}{2L} (n_x^2 + n_y^2 + n_z^2)^{1/2} \]

    Thus, from \(\nu = p c / h\), the allowed value of \(\nu\) is given by triplets \(\{n_x, n_y, n_z\}\). We now approximate the sum over all triplets as an integral in analogy with chapter 10.3. However, we also have two possible transverse polarizations of the electromagnetic wave, so an additional factor of two enters:

    \[ \sum_{\text{modes}} A(\nu) \rightarrow 2 \times 8 \int A(\nu) 4 \pi n^2 dn = 8 \times \int A(\nu) 4 \pi \nu^2 L^3 \frac{d\nu}{c^3} \]

    The density of modes \(g(\nu)\) is defined through:

    \[ g(\nu) d\nu = \text{number of electromagnetic waves in frequency range } \nu \rightarrow \nu + d\nu = \frac{8 \pi \nu^2 V d\nu}{c^3} \]

    Thus, the energy in modes \(\nu \rightarrow \nu + d\nu\) is:

    \[ \epsilon(\nu) g(\nu) d\nu = \frac{8 \pi V h \nu^3 d\nu}{c^3 \left(\exp\left(\frac{h \nu}{kT}\right) - 1\right)} \]

    And the spatial energy density is given by the Planck distribution:

    \[ u(\nu) = \frac{8 \pi h \nu^3}{c^3 \left(\exp\left(\frac{h \nu}{kT}\right) - 1\right)} \]

    Sometimes the distribution appears as a function of wavelength \(\lambda = c / \nu\), which is obtained by changing variables:

    \[ d\nu = - \frac{c}{\lambda^2} d\lambda \]

    \[ u(\lambda) d\lambda = \frac{8 \pi h c}{\lambda^5 \left(\exp\left(\frac{h c}{\lambda kT}\right) - 1\right)} d\lambda \]

    14.3 Limits of the Planck Distribution

    An important feature is that it only depends on the cavity through temperature, not through composition or shape (see Figure 1).

    Low-Frequency Limit

    First, consider the limit of low \(\nu\) when \(\frac{h \nu}{kT} \ll 1\):

    \[ u(\nu) \approx \frac{8 \pi k T \nu^2}{c^3} \]

    This is a classical result since \(h\) does not appear (it can be obtained by purely classical arguments). However, if one tries to apply the formula for all frequencies, in particular high frequencies, the “ultraviolet catastrophe” occurs, whereby the higher the frequency, the more energy is stored—essentially, a blackbody radiator would kill us all with high-frequency radiation!

    High-Frequency Limit

    The correct high-frequency limit is from the Planck distribution when \(\frac{h \nu}{kT} \gg 1\):

    \[ u(\nu) \approx \frac{8 \pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \exp\left(- \frac{h \nu}{kT}\right) \]

    In this formula, the negative exponential kills the cubic power in \(\nu\) and the “ultraviolet catastrophe” is removed. This is a direct result of using a quantum picture for the normal modes in the cavity and was one of the first successes of quantum statistical mechanics.
