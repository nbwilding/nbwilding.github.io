13. The Ideal fermi gas

Low Density Limit

Consider the limit \(e^{\mu/kT} \ll 1\), i.e., \(\mu\) large and negative. Then:

\[ n_i = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{\epsilon_i - \mu}{kT}\right) + 1} \approx e^{-\frac{\epsilon_i - \mu}{kT}} \]

for both Fermi-Dirac (F-D) and Bose-Einstein (B-E) distributions.

Now,

\[ N = \sum_i n_i \approx e^{\mu/kT} \sum_i e^{-\epsilon_i/kT} = e^{\mu/kT} Z(1) \]

where we have identified the canonical single particle partition function \(Z(1)\). Thus we see that in the limit \(e^{\mu/kT} \ll 1\):

\[ Z(1)N \ll 1 \]

which, referring to chapters 11/12, is the low density/high temperature limit where a semi-classical treatment is adequate.

In Tutorial Question 8.2 (for the mathematically inclined), it is shown how the semi-classical partition function is recovered.

Also, we can see that in the low-density limit:

\[ \mu \approx kT \ln \left( \frac{N}{Z(1)} \right) \]

which recovers the semi-classical \(\mu\) of chapter 14.3.

Ideal Fermi Gas

We return now to the Fermi-Dirac distribution from Key Point 17 (chapter 15). The function \(f_+\) is often referred to simply as the Fermi function.

Consider the limit \(T \to 0\) (\(\beta \to \infty\)):

\[ f_+(\epsilon) = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{\epsilon - \mu}{kT}\right) + 1} \to \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \epsilon < \mu_f \\ 0 & \text{if } \epsilon > \mu_f \end{cases} \]

where \(\mu_f\) is the Fermi energy defined by:

\[ \mu_f = \lim_{T \to 0} \mu(T) \]

Key Point 18: From Tutorial Question 8.3, you should convince yourself that the Fermi-Dirac distribution is equivalent to the probability that a quantum state of energy \(\epsilon\) is occupied.

In Figure 1, we observe the following features:

  • All states up to \(\mu_f\) are filled with probability 1.
  • All states above \(\mu_f\) are empty.
  • This is very different from a classical gas where at zero temperature, all gas molecules would have zero energy.
  • It is a direct result of the exclusion principle, which leads to an “effective repulsion” between fermions.

We now calculate \(\mu_f\). First, turn the sum over quantum states into an integral:

\[ N = \sum_i f_+(\epsilon_i) \approx \int_0^{\infty} d\epsilon\, g(\epsilon) f_+(\epsilon) \]

where \(g(\epsilon)\) is the density of states, a concept first introduced in chapter 11. As a reminder:

\[ g(\epsilon) d\epsilon = \text{number of states in energy range } [\epsilon, \epsilon + d\epsilon] \]

We saw in chapter 11 that for spinless particles in a box:

\[ g(\epsilon) = D \epsilon^{1/2}, \quad D = \left( \frac{2m}{h^2} \right)^{3/2} \frac{1}{4\pi^2} \]

Incorporating the idea of spin, each translational (“standing wave”) state corresponds to \(2s + 1\) states since the particle has \(2s + 1\) possible spin states. Therefore:

\[ g(\epsilon) = \tilde{D} \epsilon^{1/2}, \quad \tilde{D} = (2s + 1) D \]

Thus:

\[ N = \int_0^{\mu_f} \tilde{D} \epsilon^{1/2} d\epsilon = \frac{2}{3} \tilde{D} \mu_f^{3/2} \]

which implies:

\[ \mu_f = \left( \frac{3N}{2\tilde{D}V} \right)^{2/3} \]

or equivalently:

\[ \mu_f = \frac{h^2}{2m} \left( \frac{6\pi^2 N}{(2s + 1)V} \right)^{2/3} \]

In Tutorial Question 8.5, it is shown that:

\[ E = \int_0^{\mu_f} g(\epsilon) \epsilon d\epsilon = \frac{3}{5} N \mu_f \]

Important Points:

  • \(\mu_f\) decreases with the mass \(M\) of the fermion.
  • \(\mu_f\) increases with density \(N/V\).
  • \(\mu_f\) defines a characteristic temperature through \(\mu_f = kT_f\).
  • At zero temperature, there is a finite energy per particle \(\epsilon = \frac{3}{5} \mu_f\).

Low Temperature Behavior

Now consider the Fermi function at low but finite \(T\). The meaning of “low” will be specified shortly. Note that:

\[ f_+(\epsilon) = \frac{1}{\exp\left(\frac{\epsilon - \mu}{kT}\right) + 1} \]

approaches 1 if \((\epsilon - \mu)/kT \ll -1\) and 0 if \((\epsilon - \mu)/kT \gg 1\), with \(f_+(\epsilon) = 1/2\) when \(\epsilon = \mu\). The Fermi function is a sigmoid shape illustrated in Figure 2. It differs from the zero-temperature step-function only when \(|\epsilon - \mu| \sim O(kT)\).

---
caption: "Fermi function at low temperature"
---

For the Fermi function to retain its characteristic shape, we must have \(kT \ll \mu(T) \approx \mu_f\), implying \(T \ll T_f\). We assume that for low \(T\):

\[ \mu(T) \approx \mu_f \left( 1 - O\left( \frac{T^2}{T_f^2} \right) \right) \]

Thus, the change in \(\mu\) is second-order in \(T/T_f\) and can safely be ignored in the regime \(T \ll T_f\).

An intuitive interpretation of Figure 2 is that at low temperatures, the general scenario is similar to that at zero temperature, where all states up to energy \(\mu_f\) are filled. The difference is that states within energy \(O(kT)\) below \(\mu_f\) are vacated with some probability, and previously empty states within \(O(kT)\) above \(\mu_f\) are filled with some probability. In other words, some fermions are thermally excited above the Fermi energy.

We investigate the result of this thermal excitation by calculating the heat capacity. To avoid a complicated calculation required to get the exact result, we instead make a rough estimate (for a more careful argument, see Baierlein 9.1). We expect:

\[ E(T) - E(0) \sim N \cdot \frac{kT}{\mu_f} \cdot kT \]

i.e., the change in energy is \(N\) times the fraction of fermions excited (roughly \(kT/\mu_f\)) times the typical excitation (roughly \(kT\)).

Therefore, the heat capacity is approximately:

\[ C_V \sim \frac{E(T) - E(0)}{T} \sim \frac{Nk^2T}{\mu_f} \]

The important point is that this is linear in \(T\). This contrasts with the classical gas, where \(C_V\) is a constant (equal to \(3Nk/2\)).

Summary of Ideal Fermi Gas

  1. \(E/N \approx E_\text{classical}/N\), i.e., the gas has a large kinetic energy per molecule, even at low \(T\).
  2. \(C_V\) is small.
  3. The pressure of the gas is high and doesn’t depend on \(T\) (see Tutorial Question 8.6).
Expand for a physical application: The Electron Gas

For the low-temperature physics of the Fermi gas outlined above, we

require \(T \ll T_f\). The archetypical example is the electron gas, realized by the conduction electrons in metals. The basic idea is that alkali metal atoms (such as Na, Li, K) sit on the sites of an ordered lattice (usually body-centered cubic) and donate their outermost (valence) electron to a sea of conduction electrons. Thus, there is an array of positive ions surrounded by a sea of delocalized electrons (see Figure 3).

---
caption: "Non-localized conduction electrons forming a free electron gas"
---

It is not obvious that the conduction electrons can be modeled as an ideal Fermi gas (also known as a free electron gas) — clearly, there are electrostatic interactions between an electron and the other electrons and ions. However, one argument is that a single electron sees, on average, a neutral environment. Other factors are also relevant, which will be explored in Solid State Physics courses.

For alkali metals, \(\mu_f\) is high compared to room temperature because the mass of an electron is small, and the density of conduction electrons is relatively high (see Question 8.4). Thus, the electron gas is well modeled by the low-temperature ideal Fermi gas, and the results of the previous chapter are well borne out by experiment.
